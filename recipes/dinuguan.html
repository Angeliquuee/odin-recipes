Dinuguan

Description

Dinuguan (pork blood stew) comes from the root word dugo, or blood. This dish is so named because it is a stew made with the blood of a freshly-slaughtered pig. In this recipe, the more exotic organ meats are omitted. The stew is cooked with pork belly, cheeks and liver to make dinuguan virgins less squeamish. This is a main course or can be a snack paired with rice cakes and serves up to 4 people.

Ingredients

  • 2 tablespoons cooking oil
  • 500 grams pork belly (cut into one-inch cubes)
  • 500 grams pork cheeks (cut into one-inch cubes)
  • 1/3 cup vinegar
  • 1 whole head garlic (peeled and minced)
  • 2 thumbs-sized knobs ginger (peeled and finely chopped)
  • 2 shallots or one onion (peeled and roughly chopped)
  • 5 to 6 finger chillies
  • Fish sauce (or salt) to taste
  • Pepper to taste
  • 2 to 4 cups fresh pork blood
  • 1/4 kilogram pork liver thinly sliced

Steps

  1. Heat the cooking oil in a pan.
  2. Add the pork belly and cheeks, and cook over high heat, stirring often, until the meat is no longer pink.
  3. Pour in the vinegar. Stir.
  4. Cook, uncovered, until the vinegar has been absored by the pork, and the pork has started to render fat.
  5. Cook the pork in the mixture of cooking oil and rendered fat for a few minutes.
  6. Add the garlic, shallots (or onion), ginger and chillies.
  7. Season with fish sauce (or salt) and pepper. Stir.
  8. Pour in the blood (see notes after the recipe). Stir. Bring it to the boil (the blood will turn red to brown as it cooks).
  9. Lower the heat, cover and simmer for an hour or until the pork is done.
  10. The sauce will reduce and thicken as it cooks, don't be tempted to add water unless you want a soupy dinuguan.
  11. Taste the stew from time to time and adjust the seasonings, as needed.
  12. When the pork is tender, add the liver. Stir. Simmer another ten minutes.
  13. Garnish the dinuguan with slices of chili and scallions before serving.
  14. Serve as a main dish with rice, or as a snack with sweet rice cakes.

Notes

Note that if there are solid masses in the blood, you can press them through a strainer before adding to the pork. I didn't mind the solid masses although I mash them with my hands to make sure that there are no too large pieces
